A write-up on why **MobileViT-S** ended up as the classification backbone for the
cervical cytology pipeline — and why "what's the most accurate model" was the wrong
question to start with.

## The need for edge AI in pathology

Cervical cancer screening matters most in low- and middle-income regions, where access
to cloud GPUs or powerful on-prem clusters is limited. If AI-assisted screening is going
to be democratized, the models underneath have to be **computationally efficient without
giving up diagnostic accuracy**.

So the design target wasn't a leaderboard. It was **on-device / edge inference** —
a model that could plausibly be embedded into a digital microscope or run on a local
clinic's workstation, with no cloud dependency.

## Why MobileViT-S?

MobileViT-S (Small) is a deliberate trade-off between model size, inference latency, and
accuracy. It works because it combines the two inductive biases this task needs:

- **Convolutions (CNN bias)** — efficiently process *local* features: edges, textures,
  membrane boundaries.
- **Transformer attention** — let distant parts of the image inform each other, capturing
  the *global* shape context needed to separate subtle cellular anomalies.

For cytology crops — small, texture-heavy, where both fine local detail and overall cell
morphology matter — that hybrid held up better than a plain ViT at the same size.

### Efficiency by the numbers

| Model | Parameters | Relative size |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **MobileViT-S** | **~5.6M** | **1×** |
| ResNet-50 | ~25M | ~4× larger |
| ViT-Base | ~86M | ~15× larger |

- **Model size:** < 25 MB (FP32)
- **Inference:** real-time on standard mobile-grade CPUs or entry-level GPUs

Despite being a fraction of the size, MobileViT-S *approaches* the accuracy of these much
larger backbones on this task — which is the whole point.

## Performance and clinical relevance

Screening is a sensitivity-first problem: missing a lesion is far costlier than a false
alarm that gets a second look. On evaluation, the model reached:

- **92.6%** overall accuracy
- **98%** recall on **dyskeratotic** cells (high-grade lesions)
- **93%** recall on **koilocytotic** cells (low-grade lesions)

The high recall on high-grade lesions is the metric that actually matters for a screening
aid — those are the cases you cannot afford to miss.

### The edge advantage

Running this on-device, rather than shipping slides to a server, buys three things that
matter in a clinic:

- **Privacy** — patient data never leaves the clinic.
- **Latency** — no network round-trip; results appear as the slide is scanned.
- **Cost** — runs on affordable hardware (Jetson Nano, a Raspberry Pi with an
  accelerator, or a standard office laptop).

## Takeaway

> High-performance medical AI doesn't require massive compute. Optimizing for efficiency
> (~5.6M params) is what turns a model from a benchmark result into a *deployable*
> screening tool.

For per-instance, high-volume medical inference, the real question isn't "what's the most
accurate model" — it's "what's the most accurate model that fits the latency, privacy,
and hardware budget of the place it has to run."
